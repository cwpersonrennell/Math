    Given that the circle has a radius of 9, express \(a\) (the length highlighted in red) as a function of \(\theta\).

    SolutionThe key to answering this question is to label the radius that is adjoined to \(a\). By doing so, it becomes clear that the length of the hypotenuse is \(9+a\). Thus, we can use the Sine function to get: \[ \solve{ \sin\theta &=&\dfrac{9}{9+a}\\ \left(9+a\right)\sin\theta &=&9\\ 9\sin\theta+a\sin\theta &=&9\\ a\sin\theta &=&9-9\sin\theta\\ a(\theta)&=&\dfrac{9-9\sin\theta}{\sin\theta} } \]

    (a)What is \(x\)? Round your answer to 2 decimal places.


    (b)What is \(y\)? Round your answer to 2 decimal places.

    Solution: The key to answering this question is to treat it as two separate triangles, which each have a shared leg of \(x\). The larger triangle will have a base of \(7+y\) while the smaller will have a base of just \(y\). We can write two separate Tangent equations and use both to solve for \(x\) and \(y\): \[ \solve{ \tan(42.51^\circ)&=&\frac{x}{7+y}\\ (7+y)\tan(42.51^\circ)&=&x\\\hline\\ \tan(65.56^\circ)&=&\frac{x}{y}\\ y\tan(65.56^\circ)&=&x\\\hline\\ (7+y)\tan(42.51^\circ)&=&y\tan(65.56^\circ)\\ 7\tan(42.51^\circ)+y\tan(42.51^\circ)&=&y\tan(65.56^\circ)\\ 7\tan(42.51^\circ)&=&y\tan(65.56^\circ)-y\tan(42.51^\circ)\\ 7\tan(42.51^\circ)&=&y(\tan(65.56^\circ)-\tan(42.51^\circ))\\ \dfrac{7\tan(42.51^\circ)}{\tan(65.56^\circ)-\tan(42.51^\circ)}&=&y\\ y&\approx&5.00\\\hline\\ x&=&y\tan(65.56^\circ)\\ x&\approx&11.00 } \]

  4. Use the figure below to answer the questions.

    (a) Express \(\theta\) as a function of \(\alpha\)


    (b) If \(\alpha =67.38^\circ\), determine \(\theta\). Round your answer to two decimal places.


    (c) If \(\alpha =67.38^\circ\), determine \(x\). Round your answer to two decimal places.

    Solution: First, we need to write \(x\) in terms of \(\alpha\): \[ \solve{ \tan(\alpha) &=&\frac{12}{x}\\ x\tan(\alpha)&=&12\\ x &=&\frac{12}{\tan\alpha}\\ x &=&12\cot\alpha } \]Don't be afraid to use the reciprocal identities; it makes it a *bit* easier to keep track of and removes a fraction--always a win in my book. Next, we apply the same setup to the larger triangle: \[ \solve{ \tan(\theta)&=&\frac{12}{7+x}\\ \tan(\theta)&=&\frac{12}{7+12\cot\alpha}\\ \theta&=&\arctan\left(\frac{12]{7+12\cot\alpha}}\right) } \]Which gives us the answer to part (a). Now that we have this function, we can substitute in the value for \(\alpha\). Make sure that your calculator is in degrees. When I do, I get \(\theta \approx 45.00^\circ\) rounded to two decimal places. This gives us (b). Finall, to get (c), we use the earlier equation for \(x\): \(x=12\cot\alpha\) and substitute the given value for \(\alpha\) again to get: \(x\approx 5\).
